# Axion Dynamics During a First-Order Phase Transition

## 1. Project Overview

We study axion field dynamics during a first-order phase transition (PT). The goal is to understand how the onset of the axion mass, bubble nucleation, and percolation modify the final relic abundance compared to the standard misalignment scenario.

The system naturally separates into two physical contributions:

- **Delayed Misalignment (DM)** — homogeneous axion oscillations that begin once the mass turns on during/after the phase transition.
- **Bubble Misalignment (BM)** — inhomogeneous contributions generated by first bubble collisions that produce localized kicks (shock-like effects) in the axion field.

The long-term objective is to construct a controlled, physically interpretable, and low-parameter fit for the total relic abundance that consistently combines DM and BM.

---

## 2. Axion Dynamics

### 2.1 Equation of Motion

We evolve the homogeneous axion field using the radiation-dominated background equation:

\[
\ddot{\theta} + 3H(t)\dot{\theta} + m^2(t) \sin\theta = 0,
\]

where:

- \(\theta = a/f_a\) is the dimensionless axion field,
- \(H(t) = 1/(2t)\) during radiation domination,
- \(m(t)\) is the axion mass that turns on during the phase transition.

The cosine potential is fully retained; no small-angle approximation is assumed unless explicitly stated.

---

## 3. Modeling the Phase Transition

The phase transition is characterized by:

- Bubble nucleation rate \(\Gamma(t)\),
- Percolation time \(t_p\),
- Wall velocity \(v_w\),
- Characteristic scale \(H_*\).

The PT is not instantaneous. The axion mass evolves smoothly, and the field can begin reacting before full percolation.

For analytic modeling, we often parameterize the PT timing by \(t_p\), which acts as an effective onset scale for axion oscillations.

---

## 4. Delayed Misalignment (DM)

### 4.1 Physical Picture

In DM, the axion field remains spatially homogeneous. Oscillations begin when the effective mass becomes comparable to the Hubble scale. After oscillations begin, the energy density redshifts as matter:

\[
\rho_a \propto a^{-3} \propto t^{-3/2}
\]

in radiation domination.

The relic abundance depends on:

- Initial misalignment angle \(\theta_0\),
- Effective onset time (controlled by the PT),
- Anharmonic corrections near \(\theta_0 \sim \pi\).

### 4.2 DM Fitting Strategy

We perform ODE simulations for many combinations of:

- \(\theta_0\),
- \(H_*\),
- \(v_w\),
- PT parameters (encoded in \(t_p\)).

From these simulations, we extract the late-time energy density and construct a dimensionless ratio relative to a no-PT baseline.

We attempt to fit this ratio with a compact functional form:

\[
R_{DM}(\theta_0, t_p) = \text{anharmonic factor}(\theta_0) \times F(t_p),
\]

where:

- The anharmonic factor captures the full cosine dynamics.
- The time-dependent factor captures delayed onset scaling.

### 4.3 Current Issue

A naive instantaneous-start model (oscillations begin sharply at \(t_p\)) does not perfectly reproduce ODE data.

The reason: the phase transition is not instantaneous. The axion mass turns on smoothly, and the field partially evolves during the transition.

We are therefore developing an improved DM fit that:

- Retains minimal parameter freedom,
- Accounts for smooth turn-on behavior,
- Avoids artificial degeneracies with overall scaling parameters.

---

## 5. Bubble Misalignment (BM)

### 5.1 Physical Picture

BM arises from first bubble collisions. Bubbles with collision radius \(R_c > R_{\min}\) generate axion shock waves.

The fraction of bubbles capable of producing shocks is:

\[
f_{BM} = \int_{R_{\min}}^{\infty} p(R_c) \, dR_c.
\]

The volume-weighted fraction is:

\[
f_V = \frac{\int_{R_{\min}}^{\infty} R^3 p(R) \, dR}{\int_0^{\infty} R^3 p(R) \, dR}.
\]

We compute these fractions either from:

- Full geometry simulations, or
- Analytical approximations (e.g., Minkowski exponential distribution).

The BM contribution scales with geometric factors and an amplitude kernel that depends on PT parameters.

---

## 6. Combined Model

The total relic abundance is modeled schematically as:

\[
R_{\text{total}} = R_{DM} + R_{BM},
\]

with possible mixing prescriptions depending on:

- Volume fractions,
- Shock-wave suppression factors,
- PT kinematics.

We test different gating/mixing prescriptions to prevent artificial dominance of one component.

---

## 7. Numerical Implementation

### 7.1 ODE Solver

- Direct integration of the nonlinear equation of motion.
- Radiation-dominated background.
- Late-time energy density extracted once oscillatory regime is reached.

### 7.2 Geometry Bank

- Precomputed bubble collision distributions.
- Used to determine BM fractions and volume weighting.

### 7.3 Fitting Procedure

- Fit DM-only regime first (preferably where BM contamination is negligible).
- Extract functional dependence on \(\theta_0\) and \(t_p\).
- Then incorporate BM and test full model globally.

---

## 8. Assumptions

1. Radiation domination during the PT.
2. Homogeneous DM evolution treated via ODE.
3. BM treated through geometric fractions and effective kernels.
4. Late-time scaling extracted in the matter-like regime.
5. Initial conditions specified by a single \(\theta_0\).

---

## 9. Current Understanding

- DM scaling approximately follows expected \(t_p^{3/2}\) behavior after oscillations begin.
- Anharmonicity near \(\theta_0 \sim \pi\) is essential.
- Instantaneous-start approximation is insufficient.
- BM can dominate in certain parameter regions unless properly constrained.
- Clean separation of DM and BM regimes is crucial for stable fitting.

We are currently refining the DM-only fit using ODE simulations to construct a smooth and physically motivated onset model before reintroducing the full BM mixing.

---

## 10. Next Steps

1. Construct smooth mass turn-on parameterization.
2. Refit DM-only data with minimal additional parameter.
3. Re-evaluate identifiability between DM and BM.
4. Produce global fit with controlled degeneracies.

# 11. Redefinition of the Anharmonic Factor with Explicit \( t_p \)

## 11.1 Cosmological Normalization

We work in radiation domination with

\[
H(t) = \frac{1}{2t},
\qquad
a(t) = \sqrt{2 H_* t}.
\]

We normalize the scale factor such that the phase transition time \( t_* \) satisfies

\[
t_* = \frac{1}{2H_*},
\qquad
a(t_*) = 1.
\]

Late-time redshifting of a matter-like component is therefore

\[
\rho_a(t) \propto a^{-3}(t)
\propto t^{-3/2}.
\]

---

## 11.2 No Phase Transition (Reference Case)

Without a phase transition, the axion mass is present from early times and oscillations begin when

\[
3H(t_{\rm osc}) = M_\phi.
\]

In our dimensionless units (where \( M_\phi = 1 \)), this implies

\[
\frac{3}{2 t_{\rm osc}} = 1
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
t_{\rm osc} = \frac{3}{2}.
\]

The late-time energy density can be written as

\[
\rho_{\rm noPT}(\theta_0)
=
V(\theta_0)
\, f_{\rm anh}(\theta_0, {\rm noPT})
\left(\frac{a_{\rm osc}}{a_{\rm end}}\right)^3,
\]

where

\[
V(\theta_0) = 1 - \cos\theta_0,
\qquad
a_{\rm osc} = \sqrt{2 H_* t_{\rm osc}}.
\]

The factor \( f_{\rm anh}(\theta_0, {\rm noPT}) \) encodes the full nonlinear dynamics of the cosine potential and is extracted numerically from the homogeneous ODE solution.

---

## 11.3 Phase Transition: Delayed Onset at \( t_p \)

In the presence of a first-order phase transition, we approximate the dynamics as:

- The field is frozen until \( t = t_p \),
- At \( t = t_p \) the mass turns on,
- The field evolves thereafter according to

\[
\ddot{\theta}
+ \frac{3}{2t}\dot{\theta}
+ \sin\theta
= 0,
\]

with initial conditions

\[
\theta(t_p) = \theta_0,
\qquad
\dot{\theta}(t_p) = 0.
\]

The late-time energy density can then be written as

\[
\rho(\theta_0, t_p)
=
V(\theta_0)
\, f_{\rm anh}(\theta_0, t_p)
\left(\frac{a_p}{a_{\rm end}}\right)^3,
\]

where

\[
a_p = \sqrt{2 H_* t_p}.
\]

---

## 11.4 Definition of \( f_{\rm anh}(\theta_0, t_p) \)

From the ODE solution we compute the conserved quantity

\[
E a^3
=
E(t)\, t^{3/2},
\]

in the oscillatory regime.

We define

\[
f_{\rm anh}(\theta_0, t_p)
=
\frac{
(E a^3)_{\rm late}^{\rm PT}
}{
V(\theta_0)\, t_p^{3/2}
}.
\]

Similarly, for the reference case,

\[
f_{\rm anh}(\theta_0, {\rm noPT})
=
\frac{
(E a^3)_{\rm late}^{\rm noPT}
}{
V(\theta_0)\, t_{\rm osc}^{3/2}
}.
\]

This definition cleanly separates:

- Pure potential information \( V(\theta_0) \),
- Cosmological redshifting \( t^{3/2} \),
- Genuine dynamical distortion from friction–mass interplay, contained in \( f_{\rm anh} \).

---

## 11.5 Why Shifting Only \( a_{\rm osc} \to a_p \) Is Not Sufficient

If one assumes that the PT merely shifts the oscillation time, one would obtain

\[
\rho \propto
V(\theta_0)
\left(\frac{a_p}{a_{\rm end}}\right)^3,
\]

with the same anharmonic factor as in the noPT case.

However, this is not correct.

Because the mass turns on at finite time, the term

\[
\sin\theta
\]

interacts nontrivially with the friction term

\[
\frac{3}{2t}\dot{\theta}.
\]

This modifies:

- The effective onset of oscillations,
- The turning-point structure,
- The amount of energy stored at the first oscillation.

Therefore,

\[
f_{\rm anh}(\theta_0, t_p)
\neq
f_{\rm anh}(\theta_0, {\rm noPT}).
\]

The dependence on \( t_p \) is physical and must be extracted numerically.

---

## 11.6 Simplification Strategy: Direct \( t_p \) Binning

Instead of computing \( t_p \) from phase transition microphysics parameters such as

\[
(H_*, \beta/H_*, v_w),
\]

we promote \( t_p \) to a fundamental control parameter and define bins directly:

\[
t_p \in
\left\{
t_1, t_2, t_3, \dots
\right\}.
\]

For fixed \( H_* \), we solve

\[
\rho(\theta_0, t_p)
\quad \text{for each} \quad
(\theta_0, t_p),
\]

and extract

\[
f_{\rm anh}(\theta_0, t_p)
\]

without reference to detailed PT microphysics.

The mapping

\[
(H_*, \beta/H_*, v_w)
\longrightarrow
t_p
\]

can later be reintroduced once the functional structure of

\[
f_{\rm anh}(\theta_0, t_p)
\]

is fully understood.

---

## 11.7 Dimensionless Control Parameter

The physically relevant variable is not \( t_p \) alone, but

\[
x \equiv \frac{t_p}{t_{\rm osc}^{\rm noPT}}.
\]

This distinguishes three regimes:

1. \( x \ll 1 \): PT irrelevant (recover noPT limit).
2. \( x \sim 1 \): strong distortion of onset.
3. \( x \gg 1 \): delayed misalignment regime.

In practice, fits may be more stable when expressed as

\[
f_{\rm anh}(\theta_0, x).
\]
